A Cold-Case Dossier

Evelyn Colon

The girl who was “Beth Doe”
for forty‑four years

A fifteen-year-old from Jersey City, murdered in the winter of 1976 and left nameless for a generation — and the long, strange road of science and chance that finally gave her name back, only for the case to come undone on a question of geography.

1976 – 2021 Laurytown Road Cemetery
Weatherly, Pennsylvania

For forty-four years she was an unknown — a young woman pulled from three suitcases on a frozen riverbank, buried without a name in a pauper’s field, mourned by a town that never knew who she was. This is the full account of who she turned out to be, what was done to her, the science and luck that restored her name, and the prosecution that fell apart.

Section 01

The Girl Who Had No Name

Her name was Evelyn Colon. She was born on April 17, 1961, and grew up in Jersey City, New Jersey, in a Puerto Rican family — one of five Colon children. By late 1976 she was fifteen and pregnant; the father was her nineteen-year-old boyfriend, Luis Sierra. He was not a stranger who entered her life. Her brother Luis later testified that the two families were friends and that he and Sierra had grown up next door to one another in Jersey City. Because of the pregnancy, Evelyn’s parents had recently let her move into an apartment with him.

The quiet horror of the case is how nearly her family never looked for her at all. One day Evelyn telephoned her mother to say she felt unwell and asked her to bring soup; when her mother arrived, no one was there, and neighbors said the couple had moved away. Then, in January 1977, a letter arrived — postmarked Stamford, Connecticut — saying Evelyn had given birth to a boy named Luis Sierra Jr., that she was doing well, and that she would reach out if she ever needed anything. The family believed it, and so they did not report her missing.

When they finally tried to file a report years later, police pointed to that letter and declined. And there is a detail here that is almost unbearable: Evelyn’s brother would testify that their mother went to her grave believing Evelyn had simply cut the family off because she was angry with them. Both of Evelyn’s parents were dead before she was ever identified.

“She told her mother that if anything ever happened to her, he was likely involved.”

From the police affidavit — recalled by Evelyn’s sister
Section 02

The Discovery

It began at the edge of the Lehigh River in East Side Borough, Carbon County — about fifty miles north of Allentown — beneath an Interstate 80 bridge. It was roughly 4:30 in the afternoon on a cold, rainy December 20, 1976. A fourteen-year-old Weatherly High School freshman named Kenneth Jumper Jr. was checking a line of traps he had set along the river when he noticed shapes protruding from the snow and reeds. He approached a dark, round object propped against a rock and found himself looking at a human head, its ears and nose cut away, framed by thick dark hair. A few feet away, among the reeds, lay the body of a full-term female fetus. He ran home to Tannery Road in Kidder Township and told his older brother Richard, who came to the bank to confirm it before they told their father.

What investigators reconstructed was this. Three vinyl suitcases — two striped in red, white, and blue, the third tan with a plaid design, all the same size — had been thrown from the I-80 bridge at mile marker 272.8, apparently from a vehicle traveling west. The cases had been spray-painted black at some point, and their handles cut off. The killer most likely intended for them to land in the water and disappear; instead, two came down in the woods, and the third — holding the head and the fetus — landed on the bank, roughly ten feet from the others. Having fallen about three hundred feet, two of the cases burst open, exposing the head, the fetus, and the two halves of a torso.

Packed in with the remains were the objects that would define the case for decades: a reddish-orange chenille bedspread embroidered with yellow and pink flowers, straw or hay, and waterlogged newspaper used to wrap parts of the body. The paper was a New York Sunday edition dated September 26, 1976 — and that date did fall on a Sunday — traceable to the cluster of North Jersey counties: Bergen, Essex, Hudson, Middlesex, Monmouth, Morris, Ocean, Passaic, Somerset, Sussex, and Union. That geographic fingerprint pointed straight back toward home. No one could read it that way for forty-four years.

Section 03

What the Autopsy Found

The findings are hard, and they deserve to be stated plainly rather than evaded. Evelyn had been sexually assaulted, strangled, and shot in the neck; the gunshot was inflicted after death, and the body was then dismembered with a serrated blade. Her head, arms, and legs were severed, and her nose and ears were cut off — the detailed forensic record also lists her breasts among the parts removed and never recovered. The dismemberment was judged not the work of a surgeon, but not “haphazard” either: methodical enough to suggest someone unhurried.

The remains were taken to Gnaden Huetten Memorial Hospital in Lehighton under the local coroner, the late Robert G. Deibert, and the autopsy was performed on December 23, 1976, by Dr. Herbert J. Filinger of the Philadelphia Medical Examiner’s Office, who ruled it a homicide by manual strangulation. She was a young woman in her late teens or early twenties, between four feet eleven and five feet four, weighing 140 to 150 pounds owing to the pregnancy, with undyed dark-brown shoulder-length hair and type O blood. The fetus was a full-term girl, roughly eight to nine months along. Police believed she had been killed about twenty-four hours before discovery — around December 19 — though the wider forensic estimate spans roughly the 13th through the 19th.

It is the small, human particulars that stay with you: a two-to-six-inch scar above one heel; two moles on her face, one above the left eye and one on the left cheek, possibly developed during the pregnancy; molars extracted before her teens and old fillings, but severe untreated decay and a fractured front incisor that would have caused her real pain.

An unsolved detail

The writing on her hand

The medical examiner noted a set of pen markings on Evelyn’s left palm — the letters “WSR,” followed by a 4 or 5, then a 4 or 7. If she wrote it herself, it meant she was right-handed. No one has ever decoded what it meant.

Section 04

The Lost Years

Her fingerprints went to the FBI and matched no one. A sketch was circulated nationally. Missing-persons reports across the United States and Canada were compared and excluded, and she became “Beth Doe,” her case filed by the state police under the label Criminal Homicide — N03-27244. And then the forensic science, with a cruel irony, sent everyone in exactly the wrong direction.

Isotope analysis of her hair, teeth, and bones suggested she had been born in Central Europe and emigrated young; an early anthropological read even pointed toward Serbia or Croatia. A later examination concluded she was of Mediterranean or Italian descent. The chemistry suggested five to ten years in the United States, most likely in Tennessee or a neighboring state, with the pregnancy gestating in the Southeast. Every one of those inferences was wrong. She was a Puerto Rican girl from Jersey City — a sobering lesson in how confidently misleading even good science can be.

At least twelve missing women were checked over the years and ruled out. The most prominent false lead came in September 2019, when state police floated a connection to Madeline “Maggie” Cruz, a former foster child who had run away around 1974 and last surfaced in 1976 claiming to be pregnant; the resemblance to the reconstructions was striking — and then police found Cruz alive and well. Meanwhile the case grew an online afterlife. A Facebook group started in 2013 reached nearly two thousand followers, its administrators keeping her story circulating precisely so she would not be forgotten before she could be named. The National Center for Missing & Exploited Children released two facial reconstructions, the most recent in May 2015 — and when she was finally identified, her real high-school photographs matched the computer model with uncanny closeness.

Section 05

Giving Her Name Back

The break came from the same technology that cracked the Golden State Killer. Detective Tom McAndrew of the Pennsylvania State Police took the case over around 2004, and by 2007 he and his partner Shawn Williams had made a deliberate decision to bring it up to modern standards. McAndrew kept her list of physical descriptors posted at his desk for years, carrying it with him even after he retired to the Lehigh County District Attorney’s homicide task force. The work continued under Trooper Brian Noll, who — with the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children and the Carbon County Coroner’s Office — got the victim’s DNA profile uploaded to the genealogy databases.

On the family’s side it was almost accidental. Evelyn’s nephew, Luis Colon Jr. — born a few years after she was killed — had submitted his DNA to genealogy services hoping to connect with living family, specifically a cousin he believed Evelyn had had. Instead of relatives, he was notified that he matched a homicide victim. The family had never even heard of the Beth Doe case. The match — between the victim’s bone and the nephew on her paternal Colon line — was made in 2020, and family members then steered investigators toward the ex-boyfriend. Her identity was made public on March 31, 2021. She would have turned sixty that April.

“He lived his whole adult life thinking he got away with it.”

Luis Colon Jr. — Evelyn’s nephew
Section 06

The Accused

The accused is Luis Sierra, who was nineteen at the time and in a relationship with Evelyn; investigators say his nickname in the 1970s was “Wiso.” When charged in 2021 he was sixty-three, living in the Ozone Park neighborhood of Queens, New York — a father of two, working as a bus driver. The probable-cause affidavit drew the relationship darkly. Evelyn’s sister told authorities that Sierra was abusive and would sometimes keep Evelyn locked in the apartment, and that Evelyn had told her mother she feared him — a frightened fifteen-year-old, in effect, naming her own killer in advance.

The most telling material is Sierra’s own account, which shifted under questioning. When Trooper Noll first asked whether he knew an Evelyn Colon, Sierra said no — then, told the matter dated to 1976, said he did remember her. He claimed Colon had threatened to leave him, and that when he did not find her home one day he simply assumed she had gone to live with her mother. He denied sending the Connecticut letter. And he offered no explanation for never making any effort to find his “missing” girlfriend and their child. The interview ran some six hours, with Noll present for about two of them.

The family’s reaction was raw. At Sierra’s first hearing, Evelyn’s brother Luis called out across the parking lot — “Hey, Wiso! Why did you do that?” — and shouted, even to Sierra’s own children as they arrived, that Sierra had killed his own baby. Through the entire proceeding Sierra sat quietly, graying and partly bald; the only time he spoke was to tell a reporter, on his way out, that he was innocent.

Section 07

The Other Theory

There is a counter-theory, and it is not a frivolous one. At the April 2021 preliminary hearing, Sierra’s defense attorney called the arrest a “huge reach” and argued that police should be looking instead at a serial killer who operated in northern New Jersey at the very time of Colon’s death — a man known as the “Torso Killer” for dismembering his victims.

The alternative suspect

Richard Cottingham, the “Torso Killer”

Cottingham earned the name because he dismembered some of his victims, sometimes severing heads and hands and hiding body parts that were never found — and he was active across northern New Jersey and New York City in precisely this period. In August 1974 he kidnapped, raped, and drowned two North Jersey teenagers, Mary Ann Pryor, 17, and Lorraine Marie Kelly, 16. He has since been linked to roughly eleven murders and has claimed responsibility for as many as a hundred. He was arrested in 1980.

On paper the parallels are unsettling: the same region, the same era, a dismembered young victim, severed parts never recovered. But the theory ran against the relationship evidence, the fraudulent letter, and Sierra’s own shifting statements — and Cottingham was never charged in Evelyn’s death. The judge bound the case over to county court regardless.

Section 08

The Case Collapses

Here is the part that may sting most at her grave: no one has been convicted, and no one is currently charged. Sierra was extradited to Pennsylvania and held without bail at the Carbon County Prison in Jim Thorpe; at the April 2021 hearing the magisterial judge denied bail. Then the bail picture changed on a point of law. In 2022 he was granted $250,000 bail — the court citing the Pennsylvania Supreme Court’s December 2021 decision in Commonwealth v. Talley, which held that merely charging someone with homicide is not automatically enough to deny bail. He has been free since.

Then jurisdiction undid the case entirely. A Carbon County judge dismissed the homicide charge after investigators concluded that the murder itself most likely happened in New Jersey, not Pennsylvania — where only the remains were dumped and found — and the matter was referred across the state line. Carbon County District Attorney Michael Greek put it directly: the alleged crime most likely took place in Jersey City, so the charges were dismissed in Pennsylvania.

On the precise timing the public record genuinely conflicts. Several outlets date the dismissal to 2024, while the district attorney’s own public confirmation came in early 2025; rather than impose false precision, it is honest to say only that the charge was dismissed on jurisdictional grounds and the case handed to New Jersey. As of the most recent available reporting, no charges had been refiled against Sierra in New Jersey, and the investigation there remained open. So the man her brother confronted walks free — undone, in the end, by the fact that her body crossed a state line.

Section 09

The Grave

This is what stands at the end of her story, and it has its own arc from indignity to something close to grace. From 1976 to 1983, Evelyn and her daughter’s remains sat frozen in the Philadelphia city morgue — there was no death certificate, because she had no name. When the morgue ran out of room, she was finally buried. On August 18, 1983, nearly seven years after she was found, mother and child were interred side by side in the Carbon County Potter’s Field off Laurytown Road near Weatherly, with the Reverend John A. Naegele of Lansford conducting the ceremony. She went into the ground in a cloth-covered wooden casket with no burial vault — “a matter of money,” the funeral director later explained — which matters, because the cemetery has a high water table.

It is a genuine post–Civil War pauper’s cemetery that locals call the “Poor Farm,” and others rest there the same anonymous way. It sits between Christmas-tree farms off a quiet two-lane road in the hills, the graves marked by simple white crosses.

Then came the exhumation that changed everything. On October 30, 2007, her remains were exhumed for DNA collection — the right biological samples simply had not been taken in 1976. The funeral director, Phil Jeffries, drove the remains in a police convoy down the turnpike to Warren Hospital in Phillipsburg, New Jersey — fast enough, he later joked, that he learned what his van could do above ninety miles an hour — where Dr. Isidore Mihalakis and the forensic anthropologist Conrad Quintyn performed a second autopsy and sent tissue and a femur to a Texas laboratory.

And then they gave her something close to a real funeral. On November 1, 2007, she and her daughter were reinterred, this time in a sealed metal case inside a concrete vault. Six Pennsylvania state troopers served as pallbearers, and the Reverend Donald J. Stump Sr. of Salem UCC Weatherly, with Father Floyd Caesar and Deacon Ray Wilkinson of Our Lady of Lourdes, led the committal service before about a dozen people from the community. The grace note in all of it: the family gave the baby a name — Emily Grace. Evelyn and Emily lie together.

For years the grave was unmarked. Eventually Evelyn’s name was inscribed on a marker, after the family raised money through a fundraiser specifically to fund a proper headstone and to bring relatives scattered across the country to honor her. When a reporter visited after the identification, the grave was no longer bare: it was bursting with flowers and rubber ducks, with a small cardboard sign that read “So Sad.” In the family’s own words, the community around Weatherly had loved and tended her for all forty-five years she lay there nameless.

“You have loved Evelyn as much as we do, for all these years.”

Evelyn’s family — to the people of Carbon County

There is one last thing you may feel standing there. Evelyn’s brother Luis had been living in Stroudsburg — roughly thirty miles from where his sister’s remains were found. Some of her own people had unknowingly moved to within an hour of her grave while she still had no name.

Sources & a Note on Accuracy

This account is drawn from contemporaneous newspaper reporting and later retrospectives, principally the Times News (tnonline.com) of Lehighton, The Morning Call and lehighvalleylive.com, WFMZ-TV and WNEP-TV, NJ.com and The Philadelphia Inquirer, Pennsylvania State Police media releases, the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children, and the public Find a Grave memorial. Court details follow reporting from Sierra’s 2021 preliminary hearing and subsequent proceedings.

On fairness and open questions

Luis Sierra was charged with Evelyn Colon’s murder but was never tried or convicted; the charge was dismissed, and he has maintained his innocence. The allegations described here are exactly that — allegations — and the case remains legally unresolved.

Two points carry genuine uncertainty in the public record: the exact date the charge was dismissed (reported variously across 2024 and early 2025), and the precise window of Evelyn’s death (police favored roughly twenty-four hours before discovery, around December 19, 1976). Both are presented above as ranges rather than as settled fact.
